# Seabass population model
Size-specific, temperature-dependent, energy budget model parameterized for the European sea bass (Dicentrarchus labrax). 
The model framework incorporates individual and population level aspects based on the consumer-resource model described by Persson et al. (1998) and the temperature-dependent scaling formulation in Karås and Thoresson (1992) and Ohlberger et al. (2011).
The population level model consists of a size-structured consumer population with ontogenetic diet shifts and an environment with three resources representing zooplankton, brown shrimp (Crangon crangon) and fish.

## 202X publication
European sea bass dynamics are shaped by more than warming seas

Submitted 

Abstract
`Global changes in seawater temperatures are reshaping species distributions, driving both range contractions and expansions. In recent decades, the European seabass (Dicentrarchus labrax), a warm-temperate species, has expanded its distribution to higher latitudes in the NE Atlantic including the North Sea and adjacent coastal habitats, with unknown consequences to local food webs. We investigated the population dynamics of this range expansion using a mathematical model for consumer-resource interaction, representing seabass and three key prey resources associated with its ontogenetic diet shifts. Our model simulations suggested that a marked increase in the availability of the intermediate prey resource led to 100% increase in total seabass biomass and 300% increase in abundance. In contrast, a ~2°C rise in seasonal seawater temperature, with equal resource productivity, resulted in increases of ≤ 40%. We then compared these model predictions with a long-term, high-resolution time series (1970-2022) for European seabass in the southern North Sea. The time-series data has exceptional resolution in time as well as measured variables and therefore allowed us to evaluate whether similar patterns to the model predictions were evident in the empirical data. Key features predicted by the model, including the timing of cohort emergence, cohort length distributions, and size class abundances, were reflected in the long-term monitoring data. While rising water temperature is often emphasized as a key driver of coastal ecosystem dynamics in the Anthropocene, our findings show the importance of resource availability, particularly when predators exploit prey resources in novel habitats.`
